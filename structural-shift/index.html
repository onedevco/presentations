Not everything is disrupted

What remains defensible

  • Platforms & Infrastructure — AWS, Stripe, databases. Genuine complexity you don't want to own.
  • Network Effects — Marketplaces, social platforms. The value is in the connections.
  • Data Moats — Proprietary datasets, trained models. Temporarily defensible.
  • Regulated Industries — Where the cost of getting it wrong is catastrophic. Slower to move.
07

The generalist middle market

An extinction event

Software that's good enough for most but perfect for none is losing its reason to exist.

Component libraries. Project management tools. CRMs. ERPs. Booking systems. Inventory management.

Anything where businesses have been forcing their processes into software-shaped boxes.

Threatened categories:

  • Visual component libraries
  • Generic CRM platforms
  • Project management tools
  • Workflow automation
  • Business intelligence dashboards
08
"The death of the specification"
When generating a feature costs less than precisely specifying it, the entire approach to software delivery inverts. You don't write requirements then build—you build, react, iterate.
09

What's valuable now

The scarce resource has shifted

Old Scarcity

Ability to build software

Developer time. Technical expertise. Implementation capacity.

New Scarcity

Knowing what to build

Domain expertise. Business understanding. Iteration instinct.

Every business now has access to essentially unlimited development capacity—but most have no idea how to wield it.

  • Procurement teams still evaluating last decade's criteria
  • Most businesses don't have anyone who thinks this way internally

"The last generation of software companies"

The wrong conversation

This is not about cost reduction

The instinctive response: "We can save money on SaaS" or "We can reduce headcount."

This misses the point entirely. Cost reduction is a side effect. The real opportunity is strategic differentiation—doing things your competitors cannot do.

The Strategic Question

What becomes possible when you can build exactly what you need, in days instead of years, and change it whenever the market demands?

The financial services reality

Locked into your stack

Financial services firms are particularly constrained. Regulatory requirements mandate certain systems. Core platforms are deeply embedded. Change is expensive and risky.

But this creates a false perception that nothing can change.

A framework for action

Level One
Tactical

16
LEVEL ONE · TACTICAL

Replace the pain points

Every firm has tools that everyone hates but tolerates. Generic CRMs that don't match your sales process. Reporting tools that require hours of manual formatting. Spreadsheets doing jobs that need applications.

These can be replaced in days.

  • Client meeting prep tools — Auto-populated with holdings, recent activity, market context
  • Internal dashboards — Exactly the metrics you need, not what the vendor decided
  • Document generation — Client reports, proposals, compliance docs
  • Fee calculators — Your fee structures, not generic approximations
  • Onboarding checklists — Your process, your requirements, your edge cases
17
LEVEL TWO · OPERATIONAL

Redesign the workflows

Step back from individual tools. Look at entire processes. Where are the handoffs? The bottlenecks? The error-prone manual steps?

Most workflows were designed around system limitations, not optimal outcomes. Those constraints are now removable.

  • Client onboarding — From weeks to days. Data flows, documents route, exceptions surface automatically.
  • Regulatory reporting — Same data, fraction of the preparation time. Built for your specific requirements.
  • Investment review process — Integrated analysis, automated compliance checks, streamlined approvals.
  • Client communication — Personalised at scale, consistent in quality, efficient to produce.
18
LEVEL THREE · STRATEGIC

Create new capabilities

This is where competitive advantage emerges. Not doing the same things cheaper, but doing things that weren't previously possible.

What would you build if building wasn't the constraint?

  • Hyper-personalised client portals — Every client sees exactly what matters to them, updated in real-time
  • Regulatory agility — Adapt to new requirements faster than competitors. Turn compliance from burden to advantage.
  • Proprietary analytics — Your investment thesis, your data models, your edge—not a vendor's generic toolkit.
  • Speed to market — Launch new products and services in weeks, not years.

Regulation as advantage

New reality: Firms that can adapt to regulatory change faster gain market share. When requirements shift, you're selling while competitors are still implementing.

Bespoke tooling means regulatory changes become configuration updates, not projects.

Getting started

The real question

Who builds this capability?

This isn't about hiring developers. It's about having people who understand your business and can translate that understanding into working tools.

The most effective approach: pairing deep domain expertise with rapid development capability—even temporarily—to build the first generation and establish the pattern.

  • Option 1: Upskill existing staff who know the business
  • Option 2: Bring in capability to build alongside you
  • Option 3: Hybrid approach—external expertise, internal ownership

The goal is always institutional capability, not permanent dependency.

PART III

Repositioning the
Business Model

When development cost collapses, how do you capture value?

25

The fundamental challenge

You can't sell hours anymore

Traditional software services priced on effort: days of development, complexity of build, size of team.

When AI compresses a month of work into a day, that model falls apart. You either lie about how long things take, or you charge so little the business doesn't work.

The Insight

The value to the client hasn't decreased. A tool that saves 20 hours a week is worth the same whether it took a month or a day to build.

Decouple price from input. Tie it to output.

26

Reframe the offering

What you're actually selling

Not This

  • Days of development time
  • Lines of code
  • Technical complexity
  • Size of team

This Instead

  • Business outcomes — cost saved, revenue generated, risk reduced
  • Ongoing capability — access to rapid development as a service
  • Managed solutions — working systems they don't think about
  • Accelerated learning — capability transfer to their team
27

Market positioning

Who wins in this transition

Vulnerable positions:

  • Body shops selling developer hours
  • Large consultancies with high overhead
  • Offshore teams competing on cost alone
  • Generalists without domain expertise

Strong positions:

  • Deep vertical expertise + rapid delivery
  • Outcome-based pricing models
  • Managed services with recurring revenue
  • Trusted advisors who can build, not just advise

The rare combination: Someone who understands the domain deeply, can translate that into working software rapidly, and prices on value rather than effort.

The path forward

What this means for you

  • Think strategically — Cost reduction is a side effect. What capabilities would change the game?
  • Build institutional capability — The ability to rapidly create tailored tools is now strategic infrastructure.
